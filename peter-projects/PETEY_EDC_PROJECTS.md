Curated for a materials scientist who likes to make cool shit he can actually carry.

## 1. CNC Titanium Key Organizer
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tools needed:** CAD software, access to CNC or a machine shop, or 3D printer for prototyping  
**Materials:** Grade 5 titanium (Ti-6Al-4V) or 6061/7075 aluminum, stainless fasteners, PTFE washers

**What:** A slim, layered key organizer that replaces a bulky keyring. Two or more plates stacked with standoffs — keys go between, one bolt to open/close.

**Materials Science Angles:**
- Compare weight-to-strength of Ti vs 7075 vs 6061 — build one in each, document the difference
- Explore surface treatments: type II vs type III hard anodizing on aluminum, bead blasting vs media tumbling on titanium
- Study galvanic corrosion between dissimilar metals (Ti + SS fasteners vs Al + SS)
- PTFE or bronze washers for smooth pivoting — tribology play
- Fatigue cycling test: open/close 1,000+ times, inspect for thread galling on titanium

**EDC Factor:** Daily use, pocketable, conversation starter, actually useful.

## 2. Damascus / Layered Steel Pocket Multitool
**Difficulty:** Advanced  
**Tools needed:** Angle grinder, files, drill, belt sander, heat treatment setup (forge or kiln), basic hand tools  
**Materials:** 15N20 and 1084 high carbon steel (for damascus pattern), or stainless tool steel like S35VN if going mono-material, brass or titanium bolsters, G10/Micarta scales

**What:** A minimal pocket multitool — knife blade, bottle opener, screwdriver bit, maybe a pry bar edge — forged or laminated from patterned steel.

**Materials Science Angles:**
- Damascus welding: forge-weld alternating layers, control carbon diffusion at interfaces
- Heat treatment: austenitize, quench (oil vs plate quench), temper — hardness profile mapping across layers
- Etching with ferric chloride to reveal the pattern — electrochemical differential etch rates between high-nickel and low-nickel layers
- Edge retention testing: compare different HT outcomes with rope/cardboard cutting tests
- Scale material comparison: G10 (glass fiber epoxy) vs Micarta (linen/phenolic) vs carbon fiber — impact strength, moisture absorption, wear

**EDC Factor:** The ultimate flex tool. Fits in a pocket, looks incredible, actually functional.

## 3. Anodized Titanium EDC Pen
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tools needed:** Lathe (or send to a turner), thread cutting tools, polishing supplies, or just buy blanks and modify  
**Materials:** Grade 2 or Grade 5 titanium tubes/round stock, bronze or brass tip insert, Parker-style or G2 refill, optional copper/brass accents

**What:** A machined titanium pocket pen — twist or bolt-action — with a custom anodized finish.

**Materials Science Angles:**
- Anodizing titanium to get interference-color effects — voltage vs color mapping (build a reference chart: 12V = gold, 55V = purple, etc.)
- Study oxide layer thickness control and its effect on color stability and scratch resistance
- Thread design: pitch, fit class, galling prevention (dissimilar thread pairs like Ti body + brass insert)
- Tribology of the refill interface — friction fit vs threaded retention, thermal expansion effects on grip
- Surface finish comparisons: stonewash, bead blast, mirror polish, tumbled — how each affects grip, wear, and fingerprint resistance

**EDC Factor:** Pen goes everywhere. Pull it out in a meeting and watch people lose their minds.

## 4. Carbon Fiber + Titanium Minimalist Wallet
**Difficulty:** Intermediate-Advanced  
**Tools needed:** Carbon fiber layup supplies (resin, fabric, vacuum bag or press), CNC or waterjet for titanium plates, epoxy, basic assembly tools  
**Materials:** 3K or 12K carbon fiber twill, epoxy resin, titanium or aluminum plates, copper/brass RFID-blocking foil (optional), elastic or money clip spring steel

**What:** A 3-4 card minimalist wallet built from carbon fiber skins bonded to a titanium (or aluminum) frame, with an elastic strap or money clip.

**Materials Science Angles:**
- Carbon fiber layup orientation: 0/90 vs ±45° — how fiber direction affects stiffness and impact resistance
- Epoxy cure kinetics: room temp vs post-cure effects on Tg (glass transition temperature), hardness, and bond strength
- Titanium-carbon bonding: surface prep (anodize, grit blast, silane primer) and lap shear testing different adhesion methods
- RFID shielding layer: thin copper foil vs MuMetal — attenuation comparison at 13.56 MHz
- Moisture/humidity testing: does the epoxy-carbon interface degrade? Salt spray or accelerated aging.

**EDC Factor:** Replaces a fat velcro wallet. Looks like it costs $150. Slim enough for front pocket carry.

## 5. Sapphire Pocket Prism / Refractor Keychain
**Difficulty:** Beginner-Intermediate  
**Tools needed:** Access to optical glass, sapphire, or BK7 prism (surplus/optical supplier), small metalworking for bezel/housing, UV epoxy or mechanical retention  
**Materials:** BK7 optical glass or synthetic sapphire prism, titanium or aluminum housing, stainless split ring, optional anti-reflective coating

**What:** A small geometric prism (cube corner or triangular) housed in a machined bezel that clips to a keyring. Creates rainbows, doubles as a camera lens flare tool and fidget object.

**Materials Science Angles:**
- Refractive index comparison: BK7 (n=1.517) vs sapphire (n=1.77) vs fused silica (n=1.46) — document dispersion differences with photos
- Hardness testing scratch scale: sapphire (9 Mohs) vs BK7 (~5.5 Mohs) — real world durability over 6 months of keychain abuse
- Bezel design: press-fit vs epoxy vs threaded retention — thermal cycling to see if different CTE materials loosen over time
- Anti-reflective coating exploration: if you can source or sputter a thin film, compare transmission before/after
- Optical geometry: calculate and document the deviation angle and rainbow spread for different prism geometries

**EDC Factor:** Cheap to source the prism, impressive to make, endlessly fun. Pulls rainbows on sunny days for photos, doubles as a fidget.
